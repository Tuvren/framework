### ADR-066 Blob-Per-Scope Persistence Retained; the Operational Layer Around It Is Now Git-Faithful

- **Status:** accepted.
- **Context:**
  - `SPK-BK007` (audit finding `[C-01]`, `audit-2026-07-04-170703-post-epic-87-baseline.md`) measured the blob-per-scope storage model against its own pre-declared 50ms threshold and breached it by a wide margin at 10,000 accumulated records: postgres marginal write p95 568.14ms (~11×), sqlite `health()` p95 8,930.59ms (~9s, ~178×). The spike recommended Option B (a row-per-record/path-granular redesign) as the target architecture, staged behind an interim documented-bounds ceiling of roughly 500–1,000 records per scope, and separately flagged sqlite's top-of-range `health()`/`reclaim()` growth as an unconfirmed hypothesis (n=5, `p95≈max` at that sample size) needing its own follow-up before being treated as an algorithmic defect.
  - Issue #108 opened that follow-up with a wider mandate than the spike's own STOP condition allowed: given production-code license, could the *operational* layer around the blob-per-scope model be made Git-faithful — cost proportional to what actually changed or was asked, not to accumulated scope size — without changing the storage shape itself? Eight areas were scoped (A1–A3 postgres write-path, B1–B3 validation/health, C1 reclaim, D1 cross-scope concurrency), each with an explicit landing bar: land only where a real, reproducible before/after measurement shows a gain; close with measured reason otherwise.
  - Executed across seven milestones on `perf/git-faithful-blob-persistence` (`.constitution/reports/108-git-faithful-blob-persistence.md`), with dispositions: **B3 resolved+fixed** (M1 phase-attributed baseline, M2 bisect and fix of a real O(n²) sqlite lineage-validation term — this was the actual source of the spike's unexplained top-of-range jump, not a measurement confound); **A3 landed** (M3, postgres content-hash decode memoization); **A1/A2 closed, not landed** (M4, a correct and fully proven per-family projection-cache prototype delivered no reproducible wall-clock gain, because the re-sort it targeted was never the dominant cost); **B1 landed** (M5, `health()`/`fsck()` split); **C1 landed** (M6, reclaim single-load with a targeted survivor check plus deferred foreign keys); **B2 closed, measured reason** (M7, whole-state validation is already flat and negligible); **D1 closed, not applicable, measured** (M7, instance-per-scope binding shares nothing across scopes to serialize on).
- **Decision:**
  1. **The blob-per-scope storage model is retained.** Nothing in this ADR changes the physical storage shape either backend committed to at ADR-006/048/049/050/051: postgres still keeps one canonical-CBOR snapshot row per Scope; sqlite still keeps its row-per-record schema with a whole-database `loadState`/`validateCommittedState` pass as its full-validation primitive. No wire byte, hash byte, canonical CBOR encoding, or `RuntimeBackend` contract shape changed anywhere in issue #108's eight areas — the change is confined to *when* and *how often* existing operations run, never *what* they compute.
  2. **The operational layer is now Git-faithful in four concrete ways, each measured:**
     - **Content-hash decode memoization** (A3): postgres memoizes `{ hashHex, state }` per instance and skips `decodeSnapshot` on a hash match — "trust a hash you have already seen," the same principle a content-addressed VCS applies to unchanged blobs.
     - **`health()` as a liveness probe, `fsck()` as explicit full validation** (B1): `health()` no longer loads or validates committed state on every call — it proves connectivity and schema/migration liveness only, matching what its contract (`RuntimeBackend.health()`'s doc comment, tightened by this change) ever actually promised. The whole-state pass it used to run unconditionally is now `fsck()`, one call away, exactly as Git runs `fsck` as occasional maintenance rather than on every read.
     - **Single-load reclaim with a targeted survivor check plus deferred foreign keys** (C1): `reclaim()` no longer re-runs the full `loadValidatedState` pass a second time after sweeping; a new O(survivors) `assertReclamationSurvivorInvariants` check, backed by the schema's deferred foreign keys for every FK-coverable reference and by explicit targeted checks for the FK-uncoverable opaque-CBOR references, covers every deletion-breakable invariant the enumerated coverage table in the M6 report section proves.
     - **Memoized lineage-index validation** (M2, feeding B3's resolution): the real defect behind the spike's unexplained top-of-range growth — a per-turn-node ancestor walk to the thread root, O(n²) on a linear chain — is replaced by one shared, cycle-detecting, memoizing `TurnNodeLineageIndex` per validation pass, walked once per shared ancestor prefix regardless of descendant count. This also closed the same-shaped risk inside `validateCommittedState`'s own lineage assertions across all three backends (sqlite, memory, postgres).
  3. **The residual cost curve is recorded honestly, not rounded to zero.** Optimizing the operational layer collapsed the read/maintenance-path costs that were once superlinear or ran on every call, but it does not — and by the storage shape's own nature cannot — eliminate the costs that are proportional to blob or database size:
     - **Postgres write remains O(blob), irreducible at this storage shape.** M4's isolated micro-benchmark (`postgres-encode-family.bench.ts`) measured clone+sort — A1's entire target — at ~4–8% of `encode`'s cost; the remaining **~90–96% of `encode`'s cost at 10,000 objects is `encodeDeterministicKernelRecord`'s canonical-CBOR serialization of the entire composed snapshot**, called once per encode over the whole twelve-family object regardless of how many families a write actually touched. No per-family caching strategy can reduce this without changing what unit gets canonically encoded — i.e. without lowering blob granularity below "the whole Scope," which is exactly the Option-B question this ADR does not decide.
     - **`fsck()` / full validation remains O(N), dominated by per-record identity re-hashing.** Post-M2's fix, `validateLoadedState`'s per-record canonical-identity re-hash is the *new* dominant cost inside a full validation pass — 81.8% of `fsck()`'s wall time at 10,000 sqlite rows (921ms of 1.126s at the `health()`-pre-split measurement; the identical code now lives in `fsck()`). This is legitimate, correct, O(1)-per-record work multiplied by `n` — not an algorithmic defect — but it is real and it is why `fsck()`/`reclaim()` still take just over a second at 10,000 rows even after every fix this issue landed.
     - **Cold-cache loads remain O(N).** A1/A3's benefit is real but load-bearing on cache warmth: a host that constructs a fresh `PostgresBackend` per request, or that spreads writes to one Scope across multiple concurrent writers/instances, sees decode-memoization's benefit degrade toward zero, because every writer's memo is invalidated by every other writer's commit. The full decode cost (220.9ms of a 466.5ms write at 10,000 objects, pre-memoization) is still what a cold load pays.
  4. **This ADR does not decide the row-per-record/path-granular redesign.** `SPK-BK007`'s Option B — a persistence-model redesign that changes physical storage granularity below "one blob/database load per Scope" — remains a separate, evidence-gated decision, tracked as Epic BQ's starting input. What this ADR does is (a) establish the *optimized*-blob baseline that redesign must beat, replacing the spike's raw, pre-optimization numbers as the regression floor, and (b) bound what in-place operational optimization can achieve against that baseline: the residual curve in point 3 is not a gap this issue failed to close, it is the measured ceiling of what optimizing *around* a fixed storage shape can ever deliver. A future Option-B decision is therefore about whether that ceiling is still too low for a target workload, not about whether issue #108's optimizations were thorough enough — they were verified thorough by A1/A2's own negative result (a correct, fully proven mechanism that still could not clear the landing bar) and by B2's Amdahl-bounded closure (validation's own cost is already negligible, so cutting it further cannot matter).
- **Consequences:**
  - No `RuntimeBackend` contract, wire encoding, or hash changed. `RuntimeBackend.health()`'s doc comment is tightened (not weakened in behavior — the full pass it never contractually promised is still fully available via each backend's `fsck()`) to state what it actually proves: connectivity plus schema/migration liveness, not a decodable/semantically-valid committed blob. `fsck()` is deliberately **not** added to the `RuntimeBackend` interface — it is a maintenance capability above the kernel contract, not a syscall the kernel drives, mirroring Git's own separation between the object model's read path and `git fsck` as an operator-invoked tool.
  - Every host consuming `health()` for anything other than liveness/coherence (for example, a monitoring probe that previously relied on `health()`'s side effect of re-validating committed state) must switch to calling `fsck()` explicitly for that guarantee. No such consumer exists in this repository today — `spec/conformance` has zero `health()`-governed checks — but this is a real behavior change worth naming for any external host built against the pre-M5 contract.
  - Reference numbers a future storage-shape decision should measure against, all from the committed bench scripts this issue added or hardened (`postgres-write-latency.bench.ts`, `postgres-encode-family.bench.ts`, `postgres-cross-scope-throughput.bench.ts`, `sqlite-load-cost.bench.ts`, `sqlite-hot-path.bench.ts`): postgres warm marginal write at 10,000 objects best-case 248.374ms (down from the spike's 470.08ms), sqlite `health()` at 10,000 rows best-case ~240μs flat (down from the spike's 7,818.47ms), sqlite `fsck()`/full-validation at 10,000 rows best-case 1.098–1.126s, sqlite `reclaim()` at 10,000 rows best-case 834ms (down from the spike's 14,827.57ms). The full per-phase attribution, growth curves, and reproduction commands live in `.constitution/reports/108-git-faithful-blob-persistence.md`, which this ADR treats as its evidentiary record rather than restating.
  - **Trade-off recorded:** A3's decode-memoization win is real but not universal — it is close to a 100% hit rate for the single-long-lived-instance-per-scope access pattern the committed bench measures, and close to a 0% hit rate for a fresh-instance-per-request host or a contended multi-writer Scope. Hosts and future capacity guidance should not assume the optimized numbers above apply uniformly; they describe the favorable case, honestly bounded in the M3 report section's "Honest notes and scope boundaries."
  - This ADR supersedes `SPK-BK007`'s raw, pre-optimization curves as the operative regression baseline for Epic BQ and any future Option-B evidence-gathering; the spike itself is retained as historical record of the original measurement and its staged recommendation, not amended.
